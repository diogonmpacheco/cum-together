# Ethical Monetization Strategy

## Principle

People pay for safer infrastructure, not for access to other people.

Cum Together should not become a cam marketplace, dating funnel, attention casino, or creator tipping platform. Revenue must protect the room instead of ranking the people inside it.

## What Money Should Fund

- moderation and abuse response
- age and verification strategy
- TURN relay bandwidth when peer-to-peer fails
- privacy and security engineering
- accessibility work
- host tools and community operations
- legal, policy, and trust infrastructure
- maintenance for free or low-cost access

## Payment Provider Reality

Adult products can be rejected by mainstream payment providers even when the product is legal, consensual, and safety-focused. Stripe lists adult live-chat features and mature content for sexual gratification in its restricted businesses, and PayPal's acceptable use policy requires compliance with restrictions around sexually oriented materials or services.

Before charging money, choose a payment path that explicitly permits the actual product:

- adult-friendly or high-risk merchant processing
- clear age and consent policies before checkout
- transparent refund and chargeback handling
- no ambiguous positioning that hides the adult nature of the product
- legal review before any public paid launch

Payment policy is part of trust and safety. It should be designed early instead of patched in later.

## Good Revenue Paths

### Paid Private Rooms

Hosts pay to create larger or longer private rooms.

Why it fits:

- payment attaches to room infrastructure
- no one is paid for being more desirable
- organizers can fund safer group spaces

Guardrails:

- no paid access to a specific participant
- no tipping inside the room
- no ranking paid rooms by sexual popularity

### Membership

Members pay monthly to support the platform and access reliable hosting features.

Possible tiers:

- supporter: helps fund free access
- host: private rooms, locks, moderation tools, larger capacity
- community: shared host seats for trusted groups

Guardrails:

- membership should not buy messaging access
- membership should not boost visibility
- membership should not create follower economies

### Community Plans

Trusted organizers, sex-positive educators, queer groups, clubs, and private communities can pay for managed room infrastructure.

Why it fits:

- supports existing communities
- encourages accountable hosts
- creates revenue without public discovery feeds

Guardrails:

- clear host responsibilities
- strict minors policy
- no resale of access to individuals

### Paid Verification Or Safety Review

Verification may become necessary before public rooms or larger rooms exist.

Why it fits:

- age and safety checks cost money
- verification can protect the room

Guardrails:

- verification must not become a beauty/status badge
- verification should not expose real identity to other users
- the product must explain what is verified and what is not

### Donations And Patron Support

People who believe in the mission can support it directly.

Why it fits:

- clean incentive
- lets people fund the public good aspect

Guardrails:

- donors do not get power over participants
- no donor badges inside rooms

### Host Safety Add-Ons

Optional paid tools for hosts:

- larger room capacity
- better moderation logs
- stronger local safety automation
- room lock/admit controls
- relay bandwidth packages

Guardrails:

- core safety must not be fully paywalled
- paid tools should improve hosting, not status

## Revenue To Avoid

- tips tied to individual participants
- pay-to-message
- paid DMs or inboxes
- ranking boosts
- promoted tiles
- follower counts
- paid profile discovery
- ads based on sexual behavior
- selling data
- affiliate funnels to porn/dating platforms
- private shows as the default economic model
- creator leaderboards

These recreate the same market forces the project is trying to resist.

## Suggested Starting Model

Start with:

1. Free small rooms with strict limits.
2. Paid private room hosting for larger rooms or longer sessions.
3. Supporter membership for people who want the project to exist.
4. Community plans for trusted organizers.
5. Paid relay bandwidth only when needed and clearly explained.

Do not introduce tipping, creator payouts, DMs, promoted discovery, or paid visibility.

## Pricing Hypothesis

Early numbers to test, not final pricing:

- Free: small rooms, short sessions, basic safety.
- Host: monthly fee for private rooms, locking, admit/remove, and higher capacity.
- Community: higher monthly fee for multiple hosts and organizer controls.
- Supporter: low monthly contribution with no visibility advantage.

The pricing page should emphasize funding privacy, moderation, and infrastructure.

## Equity Questions

The product should not become safe only for people with money.

Questions to revisit:

- Should there be free rooms funded by paid private hosting?
- Should communities be able to sponsor access?
- Can trusted organizers receive discounted plans?
- Can donations subsidize safety verification for people who need it?

## Success Metrics

Healthy monetization should improve:

- room safety
- host accountability
- user trust
- retention without addiction mechanics
- reports resolved
- abuse prevented
- infrastructure reliability

It should not optimize:

- time trapped in rooms
- number of messages
- sexual performance ranking
- individual earning competition
- public profile growth
- paid attention loops

## Ethical Test

Before adding a paid feature, ask:

1. Does this sell infrastructure or access to a person?
2. Does this make some participants more visible than others?
3. Does this create pressure to perform?
4. Does this increase harassment or off-platform pursuit?
5. Would people who are often targeted or vulnerable feel safer with this feature?
6. Would the project still feel proud of this model if described plainly?

If the answer is uncomfortable, do not ship it.
